Data Service Standards

A data version of the GDS Service Standards and assessment would adapt the core principles and structure of the UK Government Digital Service (GDS) framework to focus on data products, pipelines, and services, rather than purely digital user-facing services.

The goal is to ensure that data products and services are user-focused, trustworthy, interoperable, secure, and valuable.

  • 1. Understand users and their needs

    • Identify primary data users: analysts, policymakers, external researchers, operational teams
    • Conduct user research on what data they need, how they use it, and what problems they face
    • Consider varying levels of data literacy and tool preferences

  • 2. Solve a whole problem for users

    • Don't just provide raw datasets – deliver useful outputs: dashboards, APIs, summary statistics, documentation
    • Build end-to-end pipelines that deliver value (e.g. from ingestion to insight)
    • Ensure metadata, lineage, and contextual info are available

  • 3. Make data usable and accessible

    • Provide clean, well-structured, well-described datasets.
    • Follow open data principles (if appropriate).
    • Use accessible formats (CSV, JSON, APIs) and platforms that support broad access.
    • Publish clear documentation and schemas.

  • 4. Make the data secure and respect privacy

    • Follow data protection laws (e.g. GDPR).
    • Apply data minimisation, anonymisation, or pseudonymisation.
    • Use robust access controls and audit logging.

  • 5. Choose the right tools and technology

    • Use scalable, maintainable data stack components.
    • Prefer open standards and widely used tools where possible.
    • Avoid vendor lock-in.

  • 6. Iterate and improve frequently

    • Monitor how data is used and what value it creates.
    • Regularly engage with users to gather feedback.
    • Release data updates on a reliable schedule.

  • 7. Use open standards and encourage reuse

    • Use common identifiers, taxonomies, and open standards (e.g. DCAT, W3C standards).
    • Enable integration and linking of datasets (e.g. geospatial references, date/time standards).
    • Encourage version control and reproducibility.

  • 8. Be transparent

    • Publish data quality metrics, known limitations, and methodology.
    • Document assumptions, transformations, and business rules.
    • Use open methods where feasible (e.g. code on GitHub, versioned pipelines).

  • 9. Measure performance

    • Track metrics like data access volume, time to insight, user satisfaction, data freshness, error rates.
    • Define clear KPIs for your data service: availability, quality, relevance, speed.
